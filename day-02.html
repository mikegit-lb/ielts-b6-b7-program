Skill 1 Writing Task 2 · opinion essay (40 min)

Set the ⏱ Timer to 40 minutes. Write at least 250 words. Do not edit while writing today — this is a diagnostic of your natural output.

Task 2 prompt

Some people believe that university education should be provided free of charge to all students.
To what extent do you agree or disagree?

Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.

Planning framework (use before you write · 3–4 min)

1 · Decide your position in one sentence. Agree / disagree / partly agree — but be clear.
2 · Intro: paraphrase the prompt + state your thesis + preview two ideas.
3 · Body 1: your strongest supporting reason → explain → one specific example.
4 · Body 2: address the opposing view → concede it → explain why you still hold your position.
5 · Conclusion: restate the position (new words), no new ideas.

Band 7 model answer (≈ 290 words)

It is sometimes argued that higher education should be provided free of charge to every student. While the cost to taxpayers is a legitimate concern, I largely agree that university tuition should be publicly funded, because the benefits to society outweigh the financial burden.

The strongest argument for free university education is that it widens access. When students must pay high fees, those from low-income families are often deterred from applying, regardless of their ability. This represents a waste of talent that a country can ill afford. In Germany, for instance, the abolition of tuition fees has allowed capable students to attend university on merit rather than means, producing a skilled workforce that benefits the whole economy. A society that educates its ablest citizens, irrespective of their background, is likely to be both more productive and more equal.

Opponents reasonably point out that free tuition is expensive and must be financed through higher taxation. This is true, and it would be naive to ignore the strain on public budgets. However, such spending is better understood as an investment than a cost. Graduates tend to earn more, pay more tax, and rely less on state support, so the initial outlay is gradually recovered. A well-designed system, in which the wealthiest contribute through progressive taxation, can therefore fund higher education without unfairly burdening those least able to pay.

In conclusion, although the expense of free university education cannot be dismissed, I believe the gains in social mobility and long-term economic strength justify it. Governments would be wise to treat tuition not as a private expense but as a shared investment in the nation's future.

CriterionWhy this reaches Band 7
Task responsePosition stated in the intro ("I largely agree") and held to the end. Both a supporting argument and the opposing view are developed, each with a reason and a concrete example (Germany). No idea is left as a bare statement.
Coherence & cohesionFour clear paragraphs, one idea each. Referencing is smooth ("This represents…", "This is true…", "such spending"). Linkers are varied and not mechanical (However, therefore, for instance) — no "Firstly/Secondly".
Lexical resourceLess common, natural language: deterred, a waste of talent, irrespective of background, progressive taxation, the initial outlay is gradually recovered. Precise, not thesaurus-forced.
Grammar (GRA)A mix of complex structures — relative clause ("a system, in which the wealthiest contribute…"), concessive clauses ("While the cost…", "although the expense…") — and virtually every sentence is error-free.
Turkish L1 alert. Compare your draft to the model for these predictable patterns: ① articles — write "university education" / "higher education" (no "the" for general ideas), not "the university education"; ② avoid the opener "In today's modern world…" — start with the actual argument; ③ "important problem" → "a pressing concern / a serious issue"; ④ "the governments" → "governments" (general); ⑤ don't pluralise uncountables ("educations", "informations"). Fixing articles alone often lifts GRA from 6 to 6.5.

Skill 2 Speaking · Part 1 & a taste of Part 3

Record yourself (phone voice memo). Answer each question, then compare with the Band 7 model. The rule today: AREA — Answer, Reason, Example, Add. No one-sentence answers.

Part 1 — answer all four, recorded

AREA = Answer directly · give a Reason · add an Example or detail · Add a small extra point. Aim for 2–3 sentences each.
1. Do you work or are you a student?
"I'm a student at the moment — I'm studying business administration at university. [Reason] I chose it because I've always been interested in how companies are run, [Example] and last year I did an internship at a logistics firm, which really confirmed that. [Add] I'm hoping to specialise in marketing eventually."
2. What do you enjoy most about your studies?
"Probably the group projects, to be honest. [Reason] I find I learn far more when I have to explain ideas to other people, [Example] for instance we recently built a mock business plan together and I picked up a lot just from my teammates. [Add] The lectures are useful too, but the collaborative side suits me better."
3. Is your hometown a good place to study? Why?
"Yes, I'd say it is. [Reason] It's a fairly quiet city, so there aren't too many distractions, [Example] and there are plenty of libraries and cafés where you can sit and work for hours. [Add] That said, it can feel a little isolated compared with a big capital, but for focused study it's ideal."
4. How have your studying habits changed over the years?
"They've changed quite a lot, actually. [Reason] When I was younger I used to cram everything the night before an exam, [Example] whereas now I space my revision out over a couple of weeks, which works far better. [Add] I've also started studying in the mornings, when I'm freshest."

Part 3 — a harder taste (record one)

5. Some people say that practical skills are more valuable than academic knowledge. What do you think?
"[Position] I think it depends heavily on the field, so I wouldn't say one is universally more valuable. [Reason] In trades like plumbing or coding, practical ability is clearly what employers pay for. [Example] A friend of mine skipped university, learned to code online, and now earns more than most graduates I know. [Balance] That said, academic knowledge builds the critical thinking that lets people adapt when their industry changes — and industries change constantly these days. [Conclusion] So ideally, education should combine the two rather than force a choice."
Part 3 = a mini-essay out loud. Notice the scaffold: position → reason → example → balancing view → conclusion. That structure is what lifts Part 3 from Band 6 (opinion, then stop) to Band 7 (developed, balanced).

Band 7 improvement focus

Extension is the half-band. Listen back to your recording once. Count how many answers stopped after a single sentence — each one is a lost opportunity the examiner notices immediately. In writing, check that every body idea has a reason and a specific example, not just an assertion. Band 6 lists ideas; Band 7 develops them.
