Band 7 model (≈ 175 words)

The line graph shows the percentage of the population owning a smartphone in three world regions between 2005 and 2025.

Overall, ownership rose sharply across all three regions, with East Asia overtaking North America to become the highest by the end of the period, while Sub-Saharan Africa remained considerably lower throughout despite substantial growth of its own.

In 2005, North America led with 15% ownership, well ahead of East Asia's 8% and Sub-Saharan Africa's negligible 1%. East Asia then grew rapidly, overtaking North America at some point between 2010 and 2015, and by 2025 had reached 95%, four points clear of North America's 90%.

Sub-Saharan Africa followed a markedly different trajectory. Growth remained slow until 2015, after which it accelerated, climbing from 15% to 55% in the final decade alone. Even so, this left the region roughly forty percentage points behind the other two by 2025.

Turkish L1 alert. ① "percentage points" vs "percent" — use "points" when comparing two percentages ("40 points behind"), not "40% behind" which means something different. ② Don't write "the both regions" — "both regions" takes no article.

Skill 2 Reading · factual passage (Passage 1 style)

This passage is written like a real exam's easiest passage: concrete facts, clear structure, no abstract argument. Real exam pacing for a passage like this is about 17 minutes.

The Global Spread of the Smartphone

A Few inventions of the twenty-first century have spread as quickly, or as unevenly, as the smartphone. When the first iPhone was released in 2007, it combined a telephone, an internet browser, and a computer into a single handheld device, a combination that had previously required three separate machines. Google's Android operating system followed a year later in 2008, offering manufacturers a free alternative that could be installed on a much wider range of devices, and the two platforms between them have come to dominate the global market.

B Early smartphones were expensive, placing them largely out of reach for anyone outside wealthier countries. Prices fell steadily over the following decade, driven chiefly by increased manufacturing scale and intensifying competition among device makers, which pushed companies to release cheaper models aimed specifically at price-sensitive markets. By the mid-2010s, functional smartphones could be bought for a fraction of the cost of the original iPhone.

C Falling handset prices told only half the story; the other half was infrastructure. Building a mobile phone network is considerably cheaper than laying fixed telephone lines across a country, particularly in regions with difficult terrain or dispersed rural populations. As a result, many developing countries were able to skip the landline stage of telecommunications development entirely, moving straight to mobile networks — a process often described as "leapfrogging".

D This leapfrogging effect has had consequences well beyond communication. In Kenya, a mobile banking service called M-Pesa, pioneered in 2007, allowed millions of people without access to a traditional bank account to send money, pay bills, and save small amounts using nothing more than a basic phone. The service has since been credited with lifting a measurable share of Kenyan households out of extreme poverty, and similar mobile-money systems have since spread to other countries across the region.

E Even so, adoption has not proceeded at the same pace everywhere. East Asia benefited from both a strong domestic manufacturing base and significant government investment in digital infrastructure, allowing ownership rates there to overtake even wealthy Western markets. North America and Europe, by contrast, adopted early but have since plateaued, as most of the population that can afford a smartphone already owns one. Sub-Saharan Africa has grown rapidly in relative terms but started from a far lower base, and lower average incomes combined with patchier network coverage continue to slow the pace of adoption there.

F Most analysts expect global ownership to continue climbing over the coming decade. However, given the underlying gaps in income and infrastructure investment that have driven the differences so far, the gap between the fastest- and slowest-adopting regions seems likely to persist for a good many years yet.

Notice how this passage reads: facts, dates, and named examples throughout, with almost no abstract argument or hedged opinion. That's exactly what a real Passage 1 looks like — the easiest of the three, designed to be answered quickly so you can bank time for Passages 2 and 3.

Band 7 improvement focus

Three lines, two decisions. With three data series, group them by behaviour (the two that lead vs. the one that lags), not by reading order. And notice the model's hedge — "at some point between 2010 and 2015" — when the exact crossover point isn't labelled. Guessing a precise year you can't actually read from the chart is a Task Achievement risk, not a strength.
