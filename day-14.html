Passage 1 — The Economics of Extreme Weather

A Over the past two decades, the frequency and severity of extreme weather events — floods, wildfires, prolonged droughts, and increasingly powerful storms — has risen markedly, and so, correspondingly, has their economic cost. Insured losses from weather-related disasters have grown several-fold since the 1980s, even after adjusting for inflation and the simple fact that more property now exists in vulnerable locations. Economists distinguish between two related but separate concepts here: the "loss and damage" already being incurred from events occurring today, and the far larger future risk that continued warming implies for decades to come.

B The insurance industry has responded in ways that reveal how seriously the risk is now being priced. Premiums in wildfire-prone regions of California and flood-exposed areas of the southeastern United States have risen sharply in recent years, and in several cases insurers have withdrawn from these markets altogether rather than continue underwriting policies they consider unsustainably risky. This retreat has exposed what researchers term a "protection gap" — the difference between the economic losses a disaster causes and the proportion actually covered by insurance. The gap is far wider in lower-income countries, where insurance penetration remains low, meaning households and governments alike must absorb the financial shock with comparatively little external support.

C A growing body of research argues that spending on adaptation — sea walls, flood defences, drought-resistant crops, early warning systems — is considerably more cost-effective than paying for disaster response after the fact. Some studies estimate that every dollar invested in adaptation infrastructure can avoid several dollars in eventual disaster-related losses. Early warning systems, in particular, have been credited with dramatically reducing death tolls from cyclones and floods in several South and Southeast Asian countries over the past two decades. Nonetheless, adaptation spending globally remains a fraction of what is spent annually on emissions-reduction technology, despite the immediate and measurable returns it appears to offer.

D This imbalance has become a persistent point of contention at international climate negotiations. Developing nations, which have historically contributed comparatively little to cumulative global emissions yet frequently bear the most severe consequences of a warming climate, argue that wealthier, historically high-emitting nations bear a particular responsibility to fund adaptation efforts elsewhere. Wealthier nations have periodically pledged climate finance to this end, but disbursement has often fallen well short of commitments, and disputes over how funds should be allocated regularly complicate international summits.

E A further, less-discussed dimension concerns displacement. Millions of people have already been forced from their homes by climate-related disasters, yet those displaced across borders by climate impacts currently fall outside the legal definition of a refugee under existing international frameworks, leaving many without clear legal status in the country they flee to. Several legal scholars have called for this framework to be revisited, though reaching international agreement has, so far, proven politically difficult.

F Taken together, the economic evidence increasingly favours proactive investment over reactive response. Whether political will and international cooperation can catch up with this economic logic, however, remains considerably less certain than the underlying calculations themselves.

Passage 2 — Reforming Global Fisheries

A Global fish stocks have come under mounting pressure over the past half-century, with the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization estimating that roughly a third of commercially fished stocks are now fished at biologically unsustainable levels — a marked deterioration from a small fraction just a few decades ago. Given that fisheries provide the primary source of protein for over a billion people worldwide and support the livelihoods of tens of millions more, particularly in coastal developing nations, reversing this decline carries considerable economic and humanitarian weight alongside its ecological significance.

B One widely adopted policy response is the individual transferable quota, or ITQ, system, under which a total allowable catch is divided into shares that can be bought, sold, or leased among fishers. Several fisheries managed under ITQ systems, including parts of New Zealand's and Iceland's fishing industries, have seen measurable stock recovery, as the system gives individual fishers a long-term financial stake in the fishery's health rather than an incentive to catch as much as possible before competitors do. Critics counter that quotas have, in some regions, gradually consolidated into the hands of a small number of large operators, squeezing out smaller-scale fishers.

C Consumer-facing certification schemes offer a complementary, market-based approach. The Marine Stewardship Council's blue label, found on seafood products across dozens of countries, certifies that a fishery meets defined sustainability standards, giving environmentally conscious consumers a way to reward responsible practice. However, the scheme has faced periodic criticism from researchers who argue that certification standards have, in certain cases, been applied too leniently, raising concerns that some labelled products may not be as sustainably sourced as the label implies — a criticism the certifying body disputes.

D Perhaps the most persistent obstacle to effective fisheries management is illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing, commonly abbreviated as IUU fishing, which undermines even carefully designed quota systems. Satellite-based monitoring technology, such as the publicly available Global Fishing Watch platform, has made it considerably easier to track vessel movements and flag suspicious activity, yet enforcement remains extremely difficult on the high seas, where no single national authority holds jurisdiction.

E A recent international treaty governing biodiversity on the high seas represents a significant, if still largely untested, step toward addressing this jurisdictional gap, establishing a framework for creating marine protected areas beyond national waters for the first time. Whether signatory nations will provide the funding and enforcement capacity needed to make such protections meaningful remains to be seen.

F No single measure — quotas, certification, satellite monitoring, or international treaties — appears sufficient in isolation. Most fisheries scientists now argue that meaningful recovery will depend on these approaches being combined and consistently enforced, a conclusion whose logic is considerably easier to state than to implement across the world's oceans.
