Should Nuclear Power Be Part of the Climate Solution?

A As countries search for ways to decarbonise their electricity grids without compromising supply, nuclear power occupies an unusually polarising position in the debate. Unlike wind or solar, nuclear reactors generate electricity with a carbon footprint comparable to renewables while producing power continuously, unaffected by weather or time of day — a property known as providing reliable "baseload" supply that intermittent renewables cannot match without extensive storage.

B Proponents also point to nuclear's remarkably efficient use of land. A single nuclear plant can generate as much electricity as a solar farm many times its size, an advantage that matters considerably in densely populated countries where suitable land for renewable infrastructure is scarce. Supporters further argue that a broader rollout of small modular reactors — compact, factory-built units that can be installed more quickly and cheaply than traditional large-scale plants — could substantially reduce both the cost and the construction time historically associated with nuclear power.

C Critics, however, raise concerns that proponents rarely dismiss outright but generally consider manageable. Chief among these is the unresolved question of long-term radioactive waste storage: current facilities are designed to contain waste safely for many thousands of years, a timescale that makes some scientists uneasy about guaranteeing containment across generations of political and social change. Public perception of accident risk also remains a significant obstacle, even though statistical studies consistently rank nuclear power among the safest electricity sources per unit of energy generated — safer, by most measures, than coal, oil, or even rooftop solar installation, which carries a small but real risk from installation accidents.

D A further, and arguably more decisive, objection concerns cost and timescale. Traditional nuclear plants are notorious for cost overruns and construction delays, with some recent projects in Europe and North America taking well over a decade longer than initially planned and running billions over budget. Given the urgency of the climate crisis, critics argue that capital poured into a nuclear plant that will not generate electricity for fifteen years might achieve more emissions reduction if invested instead in renewables and storage, which can typically be deployed within a few years.

E Supporters counter that this criticism, while fair for traditional large-scale reactors, may not apply to the next generation of smaller, standardised designs, which remain unproven at scale but promise significantly shorter build times. Whether small modular reactors can deliver on this promise at a competitive cost remains, for now, an open question rather than a settled fact.

F What seems increasingly clear is that the debate is unlikely to be resolved by ideology alone. Most energy analysts now argue that the optimal path forward will differ by country, depending on existing infrastructure, geography, and public appetite for risk — meaning nuclear power's role in the climate solution will likely be one part of a varied, rather than universal, energy strategy.

Band 6 → 7 lesson: Q5 and Q8 are the traps that separate Band 6 from Band 7 readers. "Storage" sits directly next to "waste" in the original text, and "diversified" means almost the same as "varied" — but summary completion from a word bank usually rewards the exact word, not a synonym of it. Always test your chosen word by reading the completed sentence back against the original phrase.

Band 7.5 model answer (≈ 300 words)

Nuclear energy's role in tackling climate change divides opinion sharply. While some regard it as an indispensable part of any realistic path to a low-carbon future, others view it as a costly distraction from cheaper, safer alternatives. This essay will examine both positions before outlining my own view.

Those who champion nuclear power point chiefly to its reliability. Unlike solar or wind, which depend on weather and time of day, nuclear plants supply constant "baseload" electricity, making them uniquely suited to meeting demand that never truly pauses. Advocates also note nuclear's remarkably efficient land use — a single plant can rival the output of a solar farm many times its size — which matters enormously in densely populated nations where land for renewable infrastructure is at a premium.

Sceptics, however, counter that these benefits are undermined by two persistent problems: cost and time. Large-scale nuclear projects are notorious for running years behind schedule and billions over budget, and given the urgency of the climate crisis, capital tied up in a plant that will not generate power for over a decade arguably represents a poor use of limited climate finance, however desirable its eventual output. Concerns over long-term radioactive waste storage, though statistically overstated relative to nuclear's genuinely strong safety record, further complicate its public acceptability.

On balance, I find the case for nuclear compelling in principle but conditional in practice. Where a country already possesses nuclear infrastructure and expertise, extending and modernising it — particularly through smaller, faster-to-build reactor designs — seems a sensible complement to renewables. Where it does not, however, the years-long lead time makes nuclear a poor first response to an urgent crisis. Rather than a universal solution, then, nuclear power is best understood as one tool among several, whose usefulness depends heavily on the country deploying it.

CriterionWhy this reaches Band 7.5
Task responseBoth views are developed with two distinct, well-explained reasons each — not a single sentence per side — before a nuanced, conditional opinion ("compelling in principle but conditional in practice") that goes beyond a simple yes/no.
Coherence & cohesionSophisticated referencing across paragraphs ("these benefits", "further complicate") and a conclusion that synthesises rather than just repeats the introduction.
Lexical resourcePrecise, natural collocations well above Band 6: an indispensable part, at a premium, limited climate finance, statistically overstated, a sensible complement.
Grammar (GRA)Consistently complex, accurate structures: non-finite clauses ("making them uniquely suited…"), conditional nuance ("Where a country already possesses… Where it does not, however…") used to structure the entire final paragraph.
Turkish L1 alert. ① Note the semicolon-free, comma-controlled complex sentences — Turkish learners often over-use "and" to chain clauses instead of subordinating with "which/where/given that". ② "nuclear energy" / "nuclear power" take no article when discussed generally — avoid "the nuclear energy". ③ Avoid "In today's modern world" as an opener; this model instead opens directly on the actual tension in the debate.

Band 7 improvement focus

Exact words, exact ideas. Today's reading punished synonym-guessing in favour of exact-phrase matching — a skill that also matters in your own writing: precise word choice ("an indispensable part" vs a vague "very important") is what separates a 6.5 essay from a 7.5 one. Precision, not just correctness, is today's theme in both skills.
