Band 7 model (≈ 170 words)

The two maps show a riverside industrial area in 2010 and the same area in 2025, after considerable redevelopment.

Overall, the area was transformed from a largely disused industrial site into a technology-focused development, with three of the four original features either converted or replaced, while the car park alone remained unchanged.

In 2010, the site was dominated by a disused warehouse and an area of vacant land, with a railway line running diagonally through the site. By 2025, the warehouse had been converted into a Startup Hub, and a Data Centre had been built on what was previously vacant land. The railway line had been replaced entirely by a cycle path, reflecting a broader shift away from industrial infrastructure.

The car park, located in the south-east corner of the site in both maps, was the only feature left unaltered, retaining both its size and position throughout the redevelopment.

CriterionWhy Band 7
Task achievementOverview names the general transformation and explicitly flags the one unchanged feature — the contrast is the real story of this map pair.
CoherenceChanged features grouped in one paragraph, the unchanged feature isolated for contrast.
Lexical resourcewas transformed, converted into, had been replaced entirely by, remained unchanged, retaining — a full range of change/no-change language.
GrammarPast perfect passive ("had been converted", "had been built") correctly marks these as completed changes relative to the 2010 starting point.
Turkish L1 alert. "in the south-east corner" — directional phrases use "in the [direction] corner/part", not "at the south-east". Also: "remained unchanged" is one clean idea — avoid the doubled "stayed the same as before as it was".

Audio script — Section 3

DR OSEI: So, tell me about progress on your app prototype.

LEO: We've settled on an idea — a study-scheduling app that syncs with a student's timetable and suggests revision slots automatically.

SARA: We were originally going to build it for all subjects, but we've decided to focus just on STEM subjects for the prototype, to keep the scope realistic.

DR OSEI: Sensible. What platform are you building it for?

LEO: Android first, since that's easier for us to test on our own phones, though we'd like to add an iOS version eventually if we have time.

DR OSEI: And your user testing — who have you tested it with so far?

SARA: Just five students in our own course so far, but we're planning to expand that to at least fifteen before the final submission.

DR OSEI: Good. Any particular challenges?

LEO: The scheduling algorithm was harder than expected — getting it to actually suggest realistic time slots based on someone's existing timetable took much longer than we budgeted for.

SARA: We also underestimated how much time the interface design would take. We're a bit behind on that now.

DR OSEI: What's your plan to catch up?

LEO: We're going to split the remaining work — I'll finish the algorithm, and Sara will focus entirely on the interface for the next two weeks.

DR OSEI: And the final presentation — have you thought about what you'll demonstrate live?

SARA: We're planning to show a live demo of someone adding their timetable and getting a suggested revision plan, rather than just showing slides.

DR OSEI: That's a strong choice — examiners always respond better to something they can see working. One last thing, when's your submission deadline?

LEO: The fifteenth of next month.

DR OSEI: Good, that gives you just enough time if you stay on track.
