Band 7 model (≈ 170 words)

The diagram illustrates the six stages involved in recycling electronic waste, from initial collection through to the recovery of usable materials.

Overall, the process begins with waste being gathered and sorted, moves through a manual and then a mechanical breakdown stage, and ends with valuable materials being separated out and prepared for reuse.

In the first stage, e-waste is collected from homes and businesses before being sorted by device type. It is then passed to workers, who manually dismantle each item and remove hazardous components such as batteries by hand.

Once dismantling is complete, the remaining material is shredded into small pieces. These fragments are then separated using magnets, which draw out metallic content, before finally being sent for refining, where the recovered metals are purified so that they can be reused in new products.

Can Machines Be Fair? The Problem of Algorithmic Bias

A Algorithms now play a role in an expanding range of consequential decisions: who is offered a loan, whose job application proceeds to interview, and, in some jurisdictions, how likely a convicted individual is judged to reoffend. Proponents argue that a well-designed algorithm, applied consistently, removes the inconsistency and prejudice that can creep into human judgement. Critics counter that consistency is not the same thing as fairness, and that a biased process applied uniformly simply produces uniformly biased outcomes.

B Bias typically enters not through any explicit instruction to discriminate, but through the data on which a model is trained. Historical records inevitably reflect the patterns of a world already shaped by discrimination, so a model trained to predict, say, "successful employees" from past hiring data will learn whatever patterns produced those past hires — including patterns rooted in bias, even when no protected characteristic such as race or gender is ever explicitly included as an input. This occurs because other variables, from a candidate's postcode to their previous employer, can act as close proxies for the very characteristics the system was never told to consider.

C A widely reported case illustrates the point. One major technology company built an experimental recruiting tool intended to screen engineering applicants automatically; the system was later found to penalise resumes containing the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club captain" — because it had learned, from a decade of historical hiring data dominated by male applicants, to associate such language with a lower likelihood of being hired. Separately, independent researchers have documented substantial disparities in the accuracy of facial recognition systems across different skin tones and genders, with error rates markedly higher for some groups than others.

D Engineers have attempted various technical remedies. Simply removing protected characteristics from the input data — an approach sometimes called "fairness through unawareness" — has proven largely ineffective, precisely because of the proxy-variable problem described above. More sophisticated approaches involve reweighting training data or adjusting decision thresholds differently for different groups. Yet a deeper, more troubling result underlies all such efforts: researchers have shown that several intuitively reasonable mathematical definitions of fairness — for instance, requiring equal false-positive rates across groups versus requiring equal overall accuracy across groups — are, except in special and rare cases, mathematically incompatible with one another. A system cannot generally satisfy every reasonable fairness criterion simultaneously; designers are forced to choose which form of fairness to prioritise, a choice that is ultimately social and political rather than purely technical.

E A related tension concerns transparency. The most accurate predictive models, typically complex neural networks, tend to be the least interpretable — so-called "black boxes" whose internal reasoning even their own designers struggle to fully explain. Simpler models are easier to interrogate but frequently sacrifice some predictive accuracy in exchange. As automated systems increasingly affect people's lives, both regulators and affected individuals have begun demanding explanations for consequential decisions, creating pressure that pulls directly against the pursuit of maximum performance.

F Some governments have responded with new legal requirements: mandatory impact assessments before deployment, and in certain cases, a right for affected individuals to request human review of an automated decision. Critics argue that enforcement of these measures lags well behind the pace at which such systems are actually being deployed, leaving considerable gaps in practice even where the law, on paper, appears reasonably robust.

G What emerges from this is a problem that resists a purely technical solution. Better mathematics can clarify the trade-offs at stake, but it cannot, by itself, settle the prior and inescapably human question of which notion of fairness a given society ought to prioritise.
