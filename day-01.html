Skill 1 Reading · one academic passage

Read the passage, then answer Questions 1–12. Today: no timer — accuracy first. From Week 2 you'll do this in 20 minutes.

The Rise of Lifelong Learning

A For most of the twentieth century, education was widely regarded as a phase of life that ended in early adulthood. A person completed their schooling, perhaps attended university, and then entered the workforce equipped with a fixed body of knowledge intended to last a lifetime. This model rested on the assumption that the skills acquired in youth would remain relevant for decades. In recent years, however, that assumption has been steadily eroded.

B The principal driver of change has been the accelerating pace of technological development. Whole industries have been transformed within a single generation, and occupations that once seemed secure have disappeared or been fundamentally altered. Economists estimate that a worker entering employment today may change careers — not merely jobs — several times before retirement. In such an environment, a static store of knowledge is of limited value; what matters is the capacity to keep learning.

C This shift has given rise to the concept of "lifelong learning": the idea that education should be a continuous process extending well beyond formal schooling. Governments have embraced the notion enthusiastically, partly because a workforce able to adapt is essential to economic competitiveness. Numerous countries have introduced schemes offering subsidised training to adults, and international bodies have declared lifelong learning a policy priority.

D Yet the practice has proved more difficult than the rhetoric suggests. Adults returning to study face obstacles that younger learners do not. Many must balance courses against the demands of employment and family, and a significant proportion lack confidence, having not studied formally for many years. Research indicates that those who would benefit most from retraining — workers in declining industries — are often the least likely to enrol, deterred by cost, inconvenience, or a belief that education is "not for them".

E Technology, the very force that created the need for continuous learning, may also help to satisfy it. Online courses allow adults to study at their own pace and in their own homes, removing some of the barriers of time and location. Critics caution, however, that completion rates for such courses are frequently low, and that learning without the structure and social contact of a classroom suits only the most self-disciplined students.

F What is clear is that the traditional boundary between a period of education and a period of work is dissolving. Whether societies can build systems flexible enough to support learning across an entire lifetime — and reach the people who need it most — remains one of the central questions of modern economic policy.

Reading — answer key

#AnswerWhere / why
1TRUEPara A: "a fixed body of knowledge intended to last a lifetime".
2FALSEPara B: workers "may change careers … several times" — contradicts "single career until retirement".
3FALSEPara C: support is "partly because … economic competitiveness" — not cultural reasons.
4FALSEPara D: those who benefit most are "the least likely to enrol".
5NOT GIVENPara E says completion rates are "frequently low" but never compares them to classroom courses. No comparison = NOT GIVEN.
6technologicalPara B: "the accelerating pace of technological development".
7subsidisedPara C: "schemes offering subsidised training to adults".
8confidencePara D: "a significant proportion lack confidence".
9locationPara E: "barriers of time and location".
10BPara A. Trap C reverses the meaning — the new world has frequent job change, not the old model.
11CPara D: "a belief that education is 'not for them'". Trap A ("satisfied") is never stated.
12BPara F: "the traditional boundary … is dissolving". Trap A overstates — "dissolving", not "disappear completely".
Band 6 → 7 lesson: Q5 is the classic Not Given trap — a Band 6 student sees "completion rates … low" and picks FALSE. But FALSE needs a contradiction; here there is simply no comparison. And Q2/Q10 punish keyword-matching: the right answer often reverses the passage's idea. Decide the meaning, not the matching word.

Audio script — Section 1

OFFICER: Good afternoon, Riverside Community Learning Centre, how can I help?

MARIA: Hi, I'd like to enrol on one of your evening courses.

OFFICER: Of course. Can I take your full name?

MARIA: Yes, it's Maria Delgado.

OFFICER: Could you spell the surname?

MARIA: Sure — D-E-L-G-A-D-O.

OFFICER: Lovely. And a contact number?

MARIA: It's 07924 — 551030.

OFFICER: 07924 551030. Now, which course were you interested in?

MARIA: Well, I was thinking about the painting course at first, but actually I'd prefer the photography one.

OFFICER: Great — photography's very popular. It runs on Tuesdays and Thursdays. A preferred day?

MARIA: Tuesday would be ideal, but I work late that day, so Thursday.

OFFICER: Thursdays it is. Classes start at six o'clock, though the photography group actually begins at half past six.

MARIA: Six thirty, perfect.

OFFICER: And what level are you — beginner, intermediate?

MARIA: Definitely beginner.

OFFICER: The standard fee for the ten-week course is £150, but as you're a student, you'd pay the concession rate of £120.

MARIA: That's good to know.

OFFICER: How did you hear about us?

MARIA: A friend told me about it — well, actually it was a colleague at work.

OFFICER: Last thing — on your first evening, please bring a memory stick to save your images.

MARIA: A memory stick, will do. Thank you!

Band 6 → 7 lesson: Section 1 answers are lost to spelling and self-correction traps, not difficulty. The speaker gives a first answer, then changes it ("actually…", "but…"). The second value is almost always correct. Underline the correction words when you hear them.

Band 7 improvement focus

Never mark an answer without explaining it. For every wrong reading answer, write one sentence naming the exact line that proves the right answer. For every wrong listening answer, name the cause: spelling, a self-correction trap, or a distractor number. This "why" habit is the single biggest difference between a Band 6 and a Band 7 test-taker — it turns random practice into pattern recognition.
