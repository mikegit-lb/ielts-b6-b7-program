Band 7 model (≈ 165 words)

The pie chart illustrates the proportion of household technology spending allocated to six categories in 2024.

Overall, mobile phones and plans accounted for the largest share of spending by a clear margin, while gaming and other miscellaneous costs together made up less than a fifth of the total.

Mobile phones and plans made up the biggest single category at 28%, followed by computers and laptops at 22%. Together, these two categories accounted for exactly half of all household technology spending.

Streaming subscriptions took a further 18%, followed by smart home devices at 12%. Gaming and the miscellaneous "other" category were the smallest contributors, at 11% and 9% respectively, jointly accounting for a fifth of total spending.

Task 2 prompt

Some people believe that children should not be allowed to use smartphones or tablets until they are teenagers.

Band 7.5 model answer (≈ 290 words)

As smartphones and tablets have become an almost unavoidable part of daily life, some argue that children should be kept away from them entirely until their teenage years. While I understand the concerns behind this view, I believe an outright ban is both impractical and, in some respects, counterproductive.

Supporters of a strict age limit typically point to the well-documented risks of excessive screen time: disrupted sleep, reduced physical activity, and exposure to inappropriate content chief among them. These are legitimate concerns, and no parent should dismiss them lightly. A blanket ban until the teenage years, however, overlooks the extent to which digital literacy has become a genuinely useful skill — children who grow up entirely unfamiliar with these devices may find themselves at a real disadvantage once they do eventually gain access, having missed years in which to develop healthy habits around their use.

A more effective approach, in my view, lies in supervised, gradually increasing access rather than an all-or-nothing restriction. Many schools now incorporate tablets into early education specifically because guided use, with clear boundaries set by a responsible adult, allows children to benefit from technology's genuine advantages — educational apps, video calls with distant relatives, creative tools — while minimising its documented harms. The key variable is not the device itself but the presence, or absence, of parental guidance around it.

In conclusion, while I share the underlying concern that motivates calls for an outright ban, I do not believe age alone is the right criterion. A more nuanced policy of supervised, age-appropriate access would better serve children than a rule that treats a twelve-year-old on the verge of secondary school no differently from a toddler.

CriterionWhy this reaches Band 7.5
Task responseA clear, held position ("impractical and counterproductive") that still fully acknowledges the opposing view's legitimacy before countering it with a specific alternative (supervised access) — not a flat rejection.
Coherence & cohesionThe conclusion synthesises rather than repeats ("I do not believe age alone is the right criterion"), and referencing is precise ("These are legitimate concerns", "The key variable").
Lexical resourcean outright ban, counterproductive, a blanket ban, at a real disadvantage, the presence, or absence, of parental guidance — natural, topic-precise, not generic.
Grammar (GRA)Sustained complex subordination: "having missed years in which to develop healthy habits" (non-finite + relative clause combined) used accurately.
Turkish L1 alert. ① "children" needs no article when general ("children should not be allowed"), not "the children". ② Avoid "the technology" when speaking generally — "technology" alone, like "education" or "information", takes no article in the general sense.
