Band 7 model (≈ 175 words)

The table shows the average number of hours per week spent on five online activities across three age groups.

Overall, younger age groups spent considerably more time on social media and gaming, whereas remote work and study peaked among 25–44-year-olds; online shopping, by contrast, varied little between groups.

Social media use fell sharply with age, from 14 hours a week among 16–24-year-olds to just 3 among those over 45. Online gaming showed a similar decline, from 8 hours down to a single hour. Streaming video followed a gentler downward slope, from 10 hours to 6.

Remote work and study did not follow this pattern: the middle age group, 25–44, recorded the highest figure at 15 hours, exceeding even the youngest group's 12. Online shopping remained the most consistent activity across all three groups, ranging narrowly between 3 and 4 hours regardless of age.

Task achievementSelected 5 of 15 possible figures per paragraph, not all — chosen because they carry the pattern (steepest declines, the one peak, the flattest row).
CoherenceAge-declining activities grouped first, then the two "different" rows (remote work's peak, shopping's flatness) contrasted together.
Lexical resourcefell sharply, a gentler downward slope, did not follow this pattern, remained the most consistent, ranging narrowly.
GrammarComparatives used with precision: "exceeding even the youngest group's 12" (not just "was higher than").
Turkish L1 alert. "varied little" / "remained consistent" — a common Band 6 error is "didn't changed much"; keep past simple ("varied", "remained") for a fixed-time-point table, don't add "-ed" twice or mix aspect.

Skill 2 Speaking · full Part 1–2–3 run

Real exam shape: Part 1 (4–5 min) → Part 2 (1-min prep + 2-min talk) → Part 3 (4–5 min). Record the whole thing in one sitting if you can — around 11–14 minutes total.

Part 1 — familiar topics: technology in daily life

1. How often do you use your smartphone each day?
"Honestly, more than I'd like to admit — probably several hours a day if I add it all up. I use it constantly for messaging and checking emails, but I've also started setting screen-time limits recently, because I noticed it was eating into time I could spend doing other things."
2. What was the first piece of technology you remember using?
3. Do you prefer texting or calling people?
4. Has technology changed the way you study or work?
"Massively, yes. When I was at school, everything was handwritten, but now I do almost all my work on a laptop, and I use apps to organise my schedule and take notes. It's made me faster, though I do think I'm slightly worse at spelling than I used to be, since autocorrect fixes most of my mistakes for me!"
5. What technology could you not live without?

Part 2 — cue card, 1 minute prep, 2 minutes talking

Cue card

Describe a piece of technology you would like to buy in the future.

You should say:
  • what it is
  • what it does
  • how much it costs
and explain why you would like to buy it.
Saved automatically.
"There's a piece of technology I've been wanting for a while now — a decent pair of noise-cancelling headphones, the kind that block out almost all background sound. Basically, they use built-in microphones to detect outside noise and then produce an opposite sound wave that cancels it out, so all you hear is your music, or in my case, usually silence while I'm trying to concentrate. The good ones I've been looking at cost somewhere around two hundred dollars, which honestly feels like a lot for headphones, but I think it's justified given how much I'd use them. The main reason I want them is that I study in fairly noisy environments — shared spaces, cafés, sometimes even on public transport — and I find it almost impossible to focus when there's chatter around me. I think they'd genuinely improve how productive I am, which, to me, makes the price worth paying in the long run."

Part 3 — abstract discussion

1. Do you think people today rely too much on technology?
"I think it depends what you mean by 'too much'. On one hand, technology has made life genuinely easier in countless ways — communication, access to information, even basic tasks like navigation. On the other hand, there's a fair argument that some of that convenience comes at a cost, like shorter attention spans or reduced face-to-face interaction. My honest view is that the technology itself isn't the problem — it's more about whether people use it deliberately or just by habit. So I'd say the concern isn't reliance exactly, but mindless reliance."
2. Should there be limits on how much technology companies know about their customers?
3. How might technology change the way we work in the next twenty years?
4. Do you think older people find it harder to adapt to new technology? Why?
