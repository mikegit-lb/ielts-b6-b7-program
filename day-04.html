Day 4 — Prediction in Listening + fluency without stopping

ListeningSpeaking≈ 65 min
Today's objective. Train two habits: predicting before a Section 2 monologue (so you're hunting for a known answer type, not reacting), and fluency — speaking for 60 seconds with no long pause. Both are pure Band 6→7 moves.

Warm-up · 5 min

Recall from memory: what were the three self-correction traps in Day 1's listening? (painting→…, Tuesday→…, friend→…). Then predict 5 words you'd expect in a talk about an adult education centre open evening.

Skill 1 Listening · Section 2 (an informational talk)

A single speaker giving information. First read Questions 1–8 and predict. Then reveal the script, TTS it (or have it read once), and answer. Play once only.

Get the audio: reveal the script, paste into phone text-to-speech, and listen without looking. Underline any word that signals a correction ("but in fact…", "even more…").

Audio script — Section 2

Good evening everyone, and thank you for coming to our open evening here at Riverside Adult Education Centre. Let me start with a little history. Many of you assume the building was once a school, but in fact this red-brick hall was built as a textile factory back in 1890, and only became an education centre in the 1970s.

We now run more than forty courses. Last year our language classes were the most heavily subscribed — even more popular than our photography courses, which had been the favourite for a decade. This term we're launching something completely new: a course in digital film-making, which we expect to fill quickly.

A practical note: the car park at the front is reserved for staff, so please use the free public car park just behind us on Mill Lane.

Now, where everything happens. Language classes are held upstairs in the Blue Room. Photography, because it needs the darkroom, runs in the Studio at the end of the corridor. The new film-making course will use the main Hall on Tuesday evenings. And our pottery classes take place in the Annexe across the courtyard — do bring an apron for those.

Listening — answer key

#AnswerTrap / note
1C — a factoryTrap A: "Many assume … a school, but in fact … a textile factory." The correction word "but" flips it.
2B — languagesTrap A: photography "had been the favourite for a decade" — past; languages are now "even more popular".
3A — digital film-making"launching something completely new … digital film-making".
4B — Mill Lane public car parkTrap A: front is "reserved for staff".
5A — Blue Room"Language classes … in the Blue Room."
6B — Studio"Photography … runs in the Studio."
7C — Hall"film-making … will use the main Hall."
8D — Annexe"pottery … in the Annexe." E Courtyard is a distractor — the Annexe is across the courtyard, not in it.
Band 6 → 7 lesson: Section 2 buries answers behind past-vs-present and correction signals ("used to be", "but in fact", "even more"). The tense tells you which fact is current. And in matching, a location mentioned near the answer (Courtyard) is planted to catch you.

Skill 2 Speaking · the 60-second no-pause drill

Fluency = speaking at length without noticeable effort. Today you train the no-pause habit that separates 6 from 7.

The rule: talk for a full 60 seconds. If you stop for more than 2 seconds or say "umm… how to say", restart the clock. Record each attempt. Do each topic until you get one clean 60 seconds.

Topics (do all three, recorded)

1. A subject you enjoyed at school.
2. A teacher who influenced you.
3. Something new you would like to learn.

Model — "A subject you enjoyed at school"

"The subject I enjoyed most was probably geography, and I think that surprised a lot of people. When I was younger I found it a bit dry — just memorising capital cities and rivers — but we had a teacher who made it come alive by linking everything to real events, like why cities grow where they do, or how climate shapes the way people live. I remember we did a project on our own town, mapping how it had changed over fifty years, and I found that genuinely fascinating. It taught me to look at the world a bit more curiously, and honestly, I still read about it now, so I suppose the interest never really faded."
Why it flows: notice there are no long pauses because the speaker keeps adding — a reason, then a memory, then a reflection. Fluency isn't about speed; it's about never running out of things to add. When stuck, ask yourself "why?" or "for example?" and keep going.

Band 7 improvement focus

Fillers, not freezes. When you need a moment, use a natural phrase — "that's a good question", "let me think for a second" — instead of freezing or saying "umm, how to say". Native-like fillers read as fluency; silence and translation-hesitation read as Band 6. Bank three fillers today and use them.

Homework / extension · ≈ 20 min

1. Re-record your weakest 60-second talk; listen for filler words ("umm", "how to say") and count them. 2. Re-listen to any listening answer you missed and write the exact word that caused the error. 3. Add 3 fillers to your Vocab list and use them tomorrow.
