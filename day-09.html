The Rise — and the Doubts — of Carbon Offsetting

A As pressure grows on governments, companies, and individuals to address climate change, carbon offsetting has become one of the most widely adopted — and most fiercely debated — responses available. The basic appeal is simple: rather than eliminating every tonne of carbon dioxide a business or a flight produces, an equivalent amount is compensated for by funding a project elsewhere that reduces or absorbs an equal quantity. Airlines now routinely offer offsetting at the point of ticket purchase, and many multinational corporations have pledged to become "carbon neutral" largely by this means.

B In principle, the mechanism is straightforward. An independent verification body assesses a proposed project — commonly reforestation, renewable energy installation, or methane capture — and, once approved, issues tradeable credits corresponding to the emissions the project is projected to prevent or remove. A company can then purchase these credits to offset its own reported emissions, in theory achieving a net-zero balance without altering its underlying operations.

C The scheme's central weakness, critics argue, lies in a problem known as "additionality". For an offset to represent a genuine reduction, the funded project must be one that would not have happened without the offset revenue. In practice, this is exceedingly difficult to verify: a wind farm may well have been built regardless, driven by falling technology costs and existing government subsidies, in which case the offset credits sold against it represent no additional benefit whatsoever — carbon accounting on paper, but not in the atmosphere.

D A further complication concerns permanence, and it applies with particular force to forestry-based offsets. Carbon stored in newly planted trees can, in principle, remain locked away for centuries — but forests are vulnerable to fire, disease, and illegal logging, any of which can release stored carbon back into the atmosphere within years rather than the decades originally promised to purchasers. Several high-profile offset projects have already been undermined in precisely this way, raising uncomfortable questions about how reliably any forestry credit can be guaranteed.

E None of this means offsetting is without any value; well-verified schemes, rigorously monitored, can fund projects that would otherwise struggle for finance. What most researchers now agree on, however, is that offsetting should never be treated as a substitute for reducing emissions at the source. It is, at best, a measure of last resort for the emissions that genuinely cannot yet be eliminated — not a licence to continue emitting exactly as before.

Skill 2 Speaking · Part 3 balanced argument

Record yourself. The task: for each question, genuinely argue both sides before giving your own view — a one-sided answer is a Band 6 ceiling in Part 3.

1. Should individuals be responsible for reducing their own carbon footprint, or is this mainly the job of governments and large companies?
"Honestly, I think responsibility has to be shared, though not equally. [Case for individuals] On one hand, if enough people change small habits — flying less, eating less meat — the cumulative effect can be significant, and individual choices do send a market signal that companies respond to. [Case for institutions] On the other hand, it seems a bit unreasonable to place the burden mainly on individuals when a relatively small number of large corporations are responsible for the vast majority of global emissions. Expecting ordinary people to solve a problem created largely by industrial policy feels like it lets the biggest polluters off the hook. [Judgement] So while I do think individual action matters and shouldn't be dismissed, I'd say governments and corporations bear the greater share of responsibility, simply because they have far more power to make structural change quickly."
Notice the explicit signposting — "on one hand… on the other hand… so while…". That structure alone lifts an answer from opinion to argument.
2. Do you think schemes like carbon offsetting actually help the environment, or are they just a way for companies to look responsible?
"I think it genuinely depends on how rigorously the scheme is run. [Sceptical case] There's a fair argument that some offsetting is little more than good PR — paying a small fee to claim 'carbon neutral' status without actually changing how a business operates can be a way of avoiding harder decisions. [Charitable case] That said, not all schemes are equal; well-verified projects that fund genuinely new renewable infrastructure can have a real impact, particularly in places that would otherwise struggle to access that kind of investment. [Judgement] My view is that offsetting shouldn't be a company's main climate strategy, but as a smaller part of a wider plan to actually cut emissions, it can be legitimate rather than just performative."
3. Some people say environmental protection should never come before economic growth. What's your opinion?
"I'd push back on the idea that it has to be one or the other. [Economic case] I understand the argument — developing countries in particular often feel that environmental restrictions are a luxury wealthier nations can afford but they can't, since growth is what lifts people out of poverty. [Environmental case] But framing it as a straightforward trade-off ignores the long-term cost of environmental damage, which tends to hit the poorest hardest and is often far more expensive to reverse than to prevent. [Judgement] So rather than choosing between them, I think the more useful question is how to design growth that doesn't rely on degrading the environment in the first place — which, admittedly, is a harder problem to solve than picking a side."
Now record your own answer to: "Do you think future generations will judge how our generation handled climate change?" Use the same structure: one side, the other side, then your judgement. Aim for 45–60 seconds.

Band 7 improvement focus

A considered judgement beats a quick opinion. Every model above follows the same shape: acknowledge one side genuinely, acknowledge the other genuinely, then commit to a position with a reason. Band 6 answers often skip straight to an opinion; Band 7 answers show the thinking that led there. Listen back to your recording — did you argue both sides, or just state a view?
