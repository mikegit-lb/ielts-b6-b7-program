Skill 1 Reading · one longer academic passage

Rewilding: Restoring Wild Landscapes

A Conventional conservation has traditionally sought to preserve ecosystems in a particular, often historical, state — protecting a forest as it currently stands, or maintaining a wetland's existing species mix. Rewilding departs sharply from this model. Rather than freezing a landscape in place, it aims to restore the natural processes that once shaped it, and then, wherever possible, to step back and let those processes run with progressively less human management. The underlying premise is that nature, given room and time, is better at building resilient, self-sustaining ecosystems than any human plan is.

B The simplest form is passive rewilding, in which land — frequently marginal farmland no longer profitable to cultivate — is simply left alone. Across parts of Europe, agricultural abandonment has already allowed scrubland and eventually forest to return without any deliberate planting. This approach is inexpensive and requires little ongoing intervention, but its outcomes are also the least predictable: without any active steering, a site may take decades to develop significant biodiversity, and the eventual result cannot be guaranteed in advance.

C A second approach centres on the reintroduction of a single keystone species — an organism whose influence on its ecosystem is disproportionate to its numbers. The best-documented case is the return of grey wolves to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, decades after they had been hunted to local extinction. Freed from unchecked browsing pressure, riverside vegetation recovered, which in turn stabilised riverbanks and altered the very course of some streams — a cascade of consequences triggered by a single reintroduced predator. Such interventions can be strikingly effective, but they depend on identifying the right species and on an ecosystem still capable of responding to its return.

D A third model, managed rewilding, sits between the two. At the Oostvaardersplassen reserve in the Netherlands, large grazing animals were introduced into a fenced area to mimic the grazing pressure once exerted by extinct megafauna, with the intention of letting the resulting landscape develop with minimal further interference. In practice, however, the reserve's managers have had to intervene repeatedly — culling animals during harsh winters when natural food supplies ran short — a compromise that has drawn sharp criticism from those who argue that any fenced, monitored reserve cannot be considered truly wild.

E Rewilding schemes of any kind also face resistance beyond ecological debate. Farmers neighbouring proposed reintroduction sites frequently object, fearing the loss of grazing land or, in the case of predator reintroductions, threats to livestock; in several regions, proposals to reintroduce lynx or wolves have been shelved after sustained local opposition. Supporters counter that with adequate compensation schemes and early community consultation, such conflicts can generally be minimised, though rarely eliminated entirely.

F Despite these tensions, interest in rewilding continues to grow, driven partly by mounting evidence of global biodiversity decline and partly by rewilding's promise of restoring ecosystems at comparatively low long-term cost. Proposals now under discussion in the Scottish Highlands would reintroduce the Eurasian lynx to help control deer numbers naturally. Whether such projects ultimately succeed will depend less on ecological science, which is increasingly well understood, than on the willingness of local communities to live alongside a wilder landscape.

Reading — answer key

#AnswerWhere / why
1FALSEPara A: rewilding "departs sharply" from conservation's aim of preserving a fixed state — the goals differ.
2TRUEPara C: recovery "stabilised riverbanks and altered the very course of some streams".
3FALSEPara B: passive rewilding "is inexpensive" — reversed, not merely unmentioned.
4NOT GIVENPara E: farmers "frequently object" — frequent is not "every". No claim of universal opposition is made.
5CPara D: Oostvaardersplassen managers "have had to intervene repeatedly".
6BPara C: a keystone species' influence is "disproportionate to its numbers".
7APara B: "the eventual result cannot be guaranteed in advance".
8CPara D: critics argue "any fenced, monitored reserve cannot be considered truly wild".
Band 6 → 7 lesson: Q4 is a scale trap — "frequently" is quietly softened by Band 6 readers into "always", producing a wrong FALSE. And in Matching Features, the answer is rarely a name-drop; it's a paraphrase of a concept ("disproportionate to its numbers" = wide-reaching effect of one organism). Match ideas, not vocabulary.

Audio script — Section 3

DR SINCLAIR: So, Priya, Tom — talk me through where you've got to with the wildlife corridor project.

PRIYA: Sure. We're looking at how green spaces connect across the city — basically whether parks and gardens form a usable corridor for urban wildlife, or whether they're too isolated from each other.

TOM: Originally we were going to look at the whole city, but that's obviously unrealistic in the time we've got, so we've narrowed it to the river corridor running through the eastern district.

DR SINCLAIR: Good — much more manageable. And your survey sites?

PRIYA: We'd planned on twelve, one every few hundred metres along the river, but Tom thinks that's too many given our timeframe.

TOM: Right, I'd rather do eight sites properly than twelve rushed ones — the data would be more reliable.

DR SINCLAIR: I'd agree with Tom there. Now, looking at your draft proposal — it's solid on methodology, but there's no mention of how you'll handle the data once you've collected it. You need an analysis plan, not just a collection plan.

PRIYA: Ah — we hadn't thought that far ahead, to be honest.

DR SINCLAIR: That's fine, just add a section. Now, for the bird species — are you planning visual counts?

TOM: That was the plan, yes, just counting what we see at each site.

DR SINCLAIR: I'd actually recommend point-count listening surveys instead — you'll pick up far more species by ear than by sight, especially with birds that stay hidden in dense cover.

PRIYA: That makes sense, we'll switch to that.

DR SINCLAIR: One last thing — I'll need the revised proposal, with the analysis plan and the listening-survey method, by the second of March. That gives you just under two weeks.

TOM: Understood, we'll get it to you well before then.

Band 6 → 7 lesson: Section 3 repeatedly sets up an original plan and then a revised plan — Q11 and Q13 both test whether you can tell them apart. The tutor's opinion, not the students' first idea, is almost always the answer when the two differ. Track whose voice is speaking, not just what's said.

Band 7 improvement focus

Track positions, not just facts. In both today's tasks, the correct answer often depends on knowing whose view it is and whether it changed. In reading, "critics argue" is not the writer's own opinion. In listening, a student's first idea is often revised by the tutor. Band 7 answers come from tracking the structure of an argument, not just spotting keywords.
