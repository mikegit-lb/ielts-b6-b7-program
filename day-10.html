Audio script — Section 4

LECTURER: Good morning everyone. Today's lecture picks up where we left off last week, looking specifically at energy storage — often called the missing piece of the renewable energy transition. The core problem is straightforward: solar and wind output is inherently intermittent, meaning generation doesn't always match demand, so any surplus needs to be stored for later use.

The most established storage method by far is pumped hydro storage, which currently accounts for roughly ninety percent of global storage capacity. It works by pumping water uphill into an upper reservoir when electricity is abundant, then releasing it downhill through turbines when demand rises. The obvious limitation is that it requires very specific geography — you need two reservoirs at significantly different elevations, which simply isn't available everywhere.

Lithium-ion batteries are the second major method, and the technology's familiar from electric vehicles. Costs have fallen dramatically over the past decade, but batteries still face two constraints: a limited operational lifespan, typically degrading after a few thousand charging cycles, and a heavy dependence on the global supply of lithium and cobalt, both of which are geographically concentrated and increasingly contested resources.

A third, less mature option is green hydrogen. This involves using surplus renewable electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen through a process called electrolysis, storing the resulting hydrogen for later use in fuel cells or turbines. Green hydrogen's chief disadvantage is efficiency — a significant proportion of the original energy is lost at each stage of conversion, so although it's promising for long-term, large-scale storage, it remains considerably less efficient than batteries for short-term use.

Governments are increasingly funding hydrogen research in the hope of bringing costs down, much as they did previously for solar panel manufacturing. But no single technology discussed today is likely to solve the storage problem alone. Most researchers now favour what's often called a diversified approach — combining several storage methods suited to different timescales and locations, rather than searching for one universal solution.

Band 7 model (≈ 185 words)

The two pie charts compare the sources of the UK's electricity generation in 2010 and 2023.

Overall, coal collapsed from a significant source to an almost negligible one, while renewables underwent the opposite transformation, becoming the single largest source by 2023. Gas and nuclear, by contrast, remained comparatively stable.

In 2010, gas was the dominant source, supplying 46% of electricity, with coal close behind at 28%. Renewables contributed only 7%, a relatively minor share, while nuclear supplied 16% and other sources made up the remaining 3%.

By 2023, this picture had been transformed. Coal's share had plummeted to just 1%, effectively disappearing from the mix, whereas renewables had surged more than sixfold to 47%, overtaking every other category. Gas fell moderately to 31%, retaining its position as the second-largest source, while nuclear held broadly steady at 15%. The "other" category edged up slightly, from 3% to 6%, but remained a marginal contributor throughout.

Band 7 improvement focus

Two data points need one story. When a Task 1 gives you two charts across time, resist the urge to write "chart 1 shows X, chart 2 shows Y" as two separate descriptions. The examiner wants the change between them named explicitly in your overview — that comparison, not the raw description, is what a two-chart task is actually testing.
