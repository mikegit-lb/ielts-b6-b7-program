The overview formula (memorise this)

"Overall, [biggest trend], while [second key feature]." Report the big movements and any crossover — never quote specific numbers in the overview. Save the numbers for the body paragraphs.

Band 7 model answer (≈ 180 words)

The line graph illustrates the proportion of adult learners enrolled in online and classroom-based courses over a twenty-year period, from 2000 to 2020.

Overall, online enrolment rose dramatically across the period while classroom enrolment steadily declined, with the two overtaking one another at around 2016. By the end of the period, online study had become by far the more popular option.

In 2000, only 5% of adults studied online, compared with 45% in classrooms. Online figures then climbed consistently, reaching 25% by 2010 before accelerating to 58% in 2020. The steepest growth occurred in the final decade.

Classroom enrolment, by contrast, followed the opposite path. Having begun at 45%, it fell gradually to 38% in 2010 and then more sharply to just 22% by 2020. The point at which online overtook classroom study fell between 2015 and 2020, after which the gap widened considerably in favour of online learning.

CriterionWhy this reaches Band 7
Task achievementA clear, separate overview naming the two key features (opposite trends + crossover) with no data. Body paragraphs then select and compare the key figures rather than listing every point.
CoherenceData is grouped by line, not described point-by-point. Contrast is signposted ("by contrast", "the opposite path").
Lexical resourceVaried trend language: rose dramatically, climbed consistently, accelerating, fell gradually / more sharply, the gap widened — not "increased/decreased" repeated.
GrammarComplex structures used accurately: participle clause ("Having begun at 45%…"), relative clause ("The point at which…").
Turkish L1 alert. ① Don't write "the number of enrolment was increased" — trends are active: "enrolment increased / rose". ② Avoid double comparatives ("more higher"). ③ Use "the proportion of" not "the proportions"; percentages are uncountable here. ④ Group data — describing every point line-by-line reads as Band 5–6, however accurate.

Skill 2 Reading · main idea & Matching Headings

Skim each paragraph, say its main idea in ≤4 words, then choose the heading. Decide the idea first — don't heading-hunt for matching words.

Technology in the Classroom

A The arrival of new technology in education is often greeted as a revolution, yet the pattern is far from new. When radio broadcasts reached schools in the 1920s, and again when television entered classrooms in the 1960s, enthusiasts predicted that traditional teaching would soon be obsolete. Each time, the promised transformation failed to arrive, and the chalkboard survived. Today's excitement about tablets and learning apps echoes those earlier hopes almost exactly.

B This is not to say that digital tools bring no benefits. A well-chosen application can give students instant access to a vast store of information and allow them to progress at their own pace, with exercises that adjust to each learner's level. For a motivated student, technology can turn a single textbook into an almost limitless library.

C The difficulties, however, are equally real. Screens are a powerful source of distraction, and long hours spent on them can leave learners tired and unfocused. More seriously, not every family can afford reliable devices or internet access, so an over-reliance on technology risks widening the gap between wealthy and poorer students rather than closing it.

D For this reason, most researchers now argue that technology should be seen as a tool rather than a replacement. A skilled teacher decides when a device helps and when it hinders, and provides the encouragement and judgement that no application can supply. The evidence suggests that the human element remains the decisive factor in whether any classroom technology succeeds.

Distractors iii and vi reuse topic words ("internet", "teachers/computers") but match no paragraph's main idea. That's the Matching-Headings trap: a heading can contain a word from the paragraph and still be wrong. Summarise the idea in your own words first, then match.

Band 7 improvement focus

Overview and main-idea are the same skill. Both ask: "what is the ONE big message here, ignoring the detail?" In Task 1 that message is your overview sentence; in Matching Headings it's the paragraph's main idea. Practise stripping away detail to find the message — it lifts both scores at once.
